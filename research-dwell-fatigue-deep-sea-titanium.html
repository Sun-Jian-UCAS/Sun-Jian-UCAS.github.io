Effects of Notches and Defects on Dwell Fatigue Mechanism and Fatigue Life of Ti-6Al-4V ELI Alloy Used in Deep-Sea Submersibles

Jian Sun, Lei Wu, Chengqi Sun. Journal of Marine Science and Engineering 9 (2021) 845.

A notch and a small surface defect both concentrate stress, but they did not produce equivalent dwell-fatigue behavior in Ti-6Al-4V ELI. This study compared smooth, notched, and drilled specimens at R = 0, then combined life data, strain histories, finite-element calculations, and microstructural observations to explain the difference.

01 · Research context

Why this problem matters

Ti-6Al-4V ELI is a candidate material for pressure hulls of deep-sea submersibles because of its strength, corrosion resistance, and low density. In service, a pressure hull experiences repeated loading with periods near maximum stress. This dwell component can introduce time-dependent plasticity and substantially alter fatigue life or failure mode.

Real components also contain geometric transitions and occasional defects. Conventional-fatigue studies had established notch and defect effects, but far less was known about whether maximum-stress dwell would amplify those effects, suppress them, or change where cracks begin. Resolving that distinction is important for transferring coupon data to components with stress raisers.

02 · Scientific question

Core question

How do a circumferential notch and a drilled surface defect differently modify fatigue life, strain accumulation, and crack-initiation mechanisms under maximum-stress dwell?

03 · Materials & methods

From loading history to local damage

The experiment was structured around three specimen classes and paired conventional/dwell loading so that local stress, defect size, and hold time could be interpreted separately.

Material

Forged and rolled Ti-6Al-4V ELI plate with approximately equiaxed alpha and lamellar transformed-beta microstructure.

Mechanical state

Average tensile strength 879 MPa and yield strength 831 MPa.

Specimens

Smooth, circumferentially notched, and smooth specimens containing one drilled hole. The notch elastic stress concentration factor was 1.3.

Defect program

Drill diameters 0.3, 0.8, and 1.8 mm with corresponding depths 0.15, 0.20, and 0.25 mm; measured sqrt(area) defect sizes covered 190-438 µm.

Loading

MTS Landmark, axial load control, room temperature in air, R = 0. Triangular conventional cycles and trapezoidal dwell cycles; rise and fall times were 2 s.

Dwell conditions

Maximum-stress holds of 2 s and 20 s were used for the notched-specimen comparison. Drilled-defect specimens were evaluated with a 120 s hold at 815 MPa.

Analysis

Extensometer strain measurement, linear-elastic FE analysis for local notch stress, SEM fractography, FIB sectioning, and EBSD.

04 · Experimental design

Designing the comparison

The notch produced a distributed local stress field, whereas the drilled hole introduced a discrete potential initiation site. Life comparisons for the notch used finite-element-derived local maximum stress, preventing nominal stress alone from obscuring the response. For defective specimens, sqrt(area) was measured from the fracture surface or from a section when failure did not originate at the defect.

05 · Key findings

What we found

01

Maximum-stress dwell did not reduce the life of the tested notched specimens.

At the studied conditions, notched-specimen lives under 2 s and 20 s dwell were comparable to conventional-fatigue lives. When compared at the same local maximum stress, notched dwell-fatigue life was higher than that of the smooth specimen. The notched specimens retained a fatigue failure mode rather than switching to a plasticity-dominated failure.

02

Dwell-fatigue life was insensitive to the tested defect-size range.

Under conventional fatigue, larger drilled defects were associated with lower life and the failures initiated from the defect. Under 120 s dwell loading at a nominal maximum stress of 815 MPa and R = 0, life did not show a systematic dependence on sqrt(area) from 190 to 438 µm, and failure was not consistently initiated at the drilled defect. The result is explicitly bounded to this material, loading, and defect range.

03

The notch response was governed by fatigue cracking, not progressive dwell strain.

Cumulative maximum strain remained nearly stable through most of the test and changed sharply only near final fracture. Differences between extensometer positions were related to the developing crack and the resulting nonuniform deformation. The 3D finite-element model helped interpret the local stress field and the sensitivity of strain measurement to crack position.

04

Facet morphology connected macroscopic failure to alpha-grain fracture.

Facet features appeared in the crack-initiation region for both conventional and dwell fatigue of notched specimens. FIB extraction followed by EBSD showed that a facet corresponded to fracture of an alpha grain. The fine line markings visible in SEM were interpreted as steps on that grain's fracture surface, while the KAM map showed locally nonuniform deformation.

06 · Failure mechanism

A staged interpretation

The comparison shows that a geometric notch and an isolated drilled defect route the specimen toward different local competition between fatigue cracking and time-dependent plasticity.

01

Local stress field

The circumferential notch distributes elevated stress around the root; the drilled hole is a discrete surface initiation candidate.

02

Maximum-stress dwell

Dwell adds time for plastic strain accumulation, but its effect depends on the specimen's competing failure mode.

03

Notched specimens

Crack initiation and growth remain dominant; life, cumulative strain, and the observed fatigue mechanism are not altered by the tested dwell periods.

04

Defective specimens

At 815 MPa and a 120 s hold, dwell reduces life but the drilled defect does not consistently control initiation over 190-438 µm; failure is classified as mixed.

05

Microstructural expression

Facet formation and stepped alpha-grain fracture connect the macroscopic crack origin to local crystallography.

08 · Impact & future direction

Why this work matters

Research significance

The results show why a label such as 'stress concentrator' is not enough for dwell-fatigue assessment. A notch and a surface defect can lead to different competition between fatigue cracking and plastic deformation, even in the same alloy. Local stress, defect geometry, and failure mode therefore need to be considered together.

Scope and limitations

The conclusions are tied to load control at R = 0, room-temperature air, the selected maximum stresses, a notch factor of 1.3, and drilled defects within 190-438 µm. The experiments did not establish a universal defect-size threshold or a complete constitutive model for dwell-induced plasticity.

Doctoral research direction

Future research could pair crystal-plasticity finite-element analysis with in-situ strain mapping to resolve how notch-root gradients, grain orientation, and dwell duration redistribute stress. A probabilistic framework could then combine microstructure, defect populations, and component geometry for deep-sea pressure-hull reliability.
