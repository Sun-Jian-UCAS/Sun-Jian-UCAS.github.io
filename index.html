Research narrative

From engineered defects to microstructural damage.

My research follows a connected question: how does a nominal loading history become a localized crack inside a real microstructure?

Across titanium-alloy studies, I have examined defect-induced very-high-cycle failure, the different dwell response of notches and drilled defects, and the evolution of slip, twinning, subgrains, and microcracks in repeatedly observed grains. I aim to extend this experimental foundation toward microstructure-sensitive and probabilistic models for structural reliability.

01
First-author paperEngineering Fracture Mechanics · 2022

Artificial surface defects in very-high-cycle fatigue

This first-author study asks why a TC17 titanium alloy containing a drilled surface defect can survive into the very-high-cycle regime and then fail abruptly. Ultrasonic fatigue testing at 20 kHz, in-situ crack observation, SEM fractography, and EBSD were combined to separate the long pre-crack interval from the rapid propagation stage. All observed cracks initiated at the artificial defect, yet the fracture surfaces lacked the fine-granular or rough-area features associated with slow internal VHCF cracking. The evidence supports a time-dependent incubation process followed by comparatively rapid crack growth.

02
First-author paperJournal of Marine Science and Engineering · 2021

Notches, defects, and dwell fatigue in deep-sea titanium

This first-author study separates geometric stress concentration from defect sensitivity in Ti-6Al-4V ELI under conventional and maximum-stress dwell fatigue. Smooth, circumferentially notched, and drilled-defect specimens were tested under load control, then interpreted using cumulative-strain measurements, finite-element local-stress analysis, SEM, FIB, and EBSD. The tested notch retained a fatigue-dominated response and showed no dwell-life reduction. At 815 MPa, R = 0, and a 120 s hold, defective-specimen life had no systematic dependence on drilled-hole size over the investigated range, and failure was not consistently defect-initiated.

03
Second-author paperInternational Journal of Fatigue · 2023

Quasi in-situ EBSD of fatigue damage evolution

This second-author collaborative study develops a quasi in-situ EBSD workflow that repeatedly returns a fatigue specimen to the same labelled surface regions after controlled loading intervals. Applied to Ti-6Al-4V ELI under cyclic loading and 10 s or 120 s maximum-stress dwell, the method tracks slip, deformation twinning, lattice misorientation, subgrain formation, grain refinement, and microcracking before final failure. The observations connect crystallographic orientation to local damage and show how increasing dwell-driven plastic strain changes the competition between brittle microcrack growth and ductile failure.
